Benjamin Franklin

Scientist, Inventor, Statesman

Biography

Benjamin Franklin was born on January 17th, 1706, in Boston, the fifteenth child in the family of an English immigrant soapmaker. His father had modest means but encouraged Benjamin’s natural curiosity and love of learning. From the age of ten, young Benjamin began working for his father. Although he was not passionate about this work, he remained a dedicated reader. He often borrowed books and sometimes managed to read an entire book in a single night. His choice of reading was extraordinary; at the age of twelve, he read works by John Locke, and later explored Plutarch and Daniel Defoe. By the age of fifteen, Franklin began writing his own articles and secretly published them in his brother’s newspaper. The articles brought success to the publication, but when his brother discovered the author, he reacted negatively. After their disagreement, Benjamin left Boston. Before turning twenty, Franklin moved to London, where he worked as a printer and published his first book. Upon returning to America, he founded a philosophical club that met weekly to discuss literature, practice public speaking, and improve moral development. This club later inspired the creation of a public library.

Achievements

  • Contributions to science and society
  • Invented the lightning rod
  • Invented bifocal glasses
  • Improved lighting systems
  • Founded one of the first public libraries in America
  • Helped establish a university
  • Advanced civic projects and public discussions

Philosophy and Personality

Franklin valued education, self-improvement, and the spread of knowledge. He believed that learning should be accessible to everyone and actively promoted public education. He founded discussion circles where people met weekly to exchange ideas, practice public speaking, and work on moral improvement. His dedication to curiosity and personal growth made him one of the most influential thinkers of his era.

Legacy

Franklin’s legacy extends far beyond his inventions and political contributions. His life reflects the values of curiosity, creativity, and service to society. His portrait appears on the one-hundred-dollar bill, but he was far more than a face on money. Until his death on April 17th, 1790, at the age of 84, Franklin remained deeply engaged in science, reading, and civic life. His influence continues to inspire generations worldwide.
